# harmony

Given equal temperment and 7-note scales...

A scale is a set of notes building on a root with a certain quality (tonic + mode). Given a key, a scale plays a horizontally functional role relative to that key, and that is what is indicated in analysis symbols. Scales are generative, and its notes may be combined in all sorts of ways. A chord is explicit about which notes of a scale are voiced together (leaving _how_ they are voiced to the staff). Chords are descriptive. Looking at a lead sheet and converting chords back to scales (while taking into account chord-specific avoid notes), that's just opening it up for more improvisational possibilities.

Given a scale, there are multiple different chords that can be voiced, defined by which scale degrees are treated as functional. 1, 3, 5, 7 degrees are commonly functional, but 2, 4, and 6 can be functional too, and those degrees (under 8) imply that 3 and/or 7 might not be played. If they are understood as 9, 11, or 13, they are valid color/tensions added on top of some kind of 7th. Turns out with all the various chord types, all the possibilities of the scale are covered, even if the standard naming scheme is awkward for historical reasons. 

To move between voiced chords, use voice-leading: stepwise motion, avoiding parallel fifths, etc, all the counterpoint stuff. 

To move between scales, it turns out there are 4 types of transitions: circle of 5/4ths, dominant to sub-dominant and vice versa, step-wise resolution of chord tones (for now, the target has to be in the triad), and "morphs" where there is a shared root or 3rd. This is what is meant by "functional" (and those last two rules do privilege 3rds, which could be expanded)

Avoid notes are minor seconds or tritones with chord tones—they can be used, but they destablize those notes and prevent them from being functional. A more fundamental way to say this is that they are notes with a lower partial than the one that is supposed to be in the chord, so they will change its meaning. (although I guess avoiding 3rds in sus chords doesn't follow that)
